What is Rabies?

Rabies is a deadly viral disease that attacks the central nervous system, transmitted primarily through the bite of infected animals. The virus travels from the wound to the brain, causing inflammation and leading to severe neurological symptoms.

  • Affects mammals including humans, dogs, bats, and wildlife
  • Nearly 100% fatal once symptoms appear
  • Preventable through vaccination and prompt treatment

Symptoms & Stages

Rabies progresses through distinct stages, each with characteristic symptoms. Early recognition is crucial for effective intervention.

  • Incubation: 3-12 weeks, no symptoms
  • Prodromal: Fever, headache, nausea
  • Acute Neurologic: Anxiety, confusion, hydrophobia
  • Coma & Death: Within days of symptom onset

Prevention & Protection

Effective rabies prevention involves multiple layers of protection for both humans and animals.

  • Vaccinate pets regularly
  • Avoid contact with wild animals
  • Seek immediate medical care after bites
  • Educate communities about risks
  • Use PEP when exposed

Transmission & Risk Factors

Understanding how rabies spreads helps in prevention and risk assessment.

  • Animal bites (95% of cases)
  • Scratches from infected animals
  • Contact with infected saliva
  • High-risk areas with low vaccination
  • Occupational exposure (veterinarians)

Emergency Response Guide

If you've been bitten or exposed to a potentially rabid animal, follow these steps immediately. Time is critical in preventing rabies infection.

1
Wash wound thoroughly with soap and water for 15 minutes
2
Apply antiseptic like povidone-iodine to the wound
3
Seek medical attention immediately for PEP assessment
4
Report the incident to local animal control authorities
